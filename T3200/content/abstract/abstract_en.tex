\begin{abstract}
The lack of medical training data limits the training of AI models in radiology. Synthetic data offer a possible solution. This thesis investigates whether a diffusion model can generate realistic synthetic 3D brain MRIs. Based on the BraTS 2023 dataset an unconditional baseline model is built, analysed in ablation studies and compared across four sampling methods. In addition, a two-stage class-to-mask-to-MRI pipeline is developed that generates fully synthetic volumes containing a tumor. Evaluation is performed quantitatively (FID, SSIM, PSNR, LPIPS) and qualitatively against the real dataset. The working models reach FID values around 25 and produce visually plausible, anatomically brain-like volumes. The pipeline matches the unconditional baseline. The main limitation is a systematic underestimation of tumor size by roughly a factor of two. Overall, the thesis shows that diffusion-based 3D synthesis is a realistic way to mitigate the data shortage, provided such biases are addressed.
\end{abstract}
